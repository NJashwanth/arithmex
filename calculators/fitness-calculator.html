Understanding BMI (Body Mass Index)

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a widely used screening tool that estimates body fat based on height and weight. It is calculated as: BMI = weight (kg) / height² (m²). The World Health Organization classifies BMI into categories: underweight (below 18.5), normal weight (18.5–24.9), overweight (25–29.9), and obese (30 and above).

While BMI is a useful population-level indicator, it has limitations. It does not distinguish between muscle mass and fat mass, meaning highly muscular individuals may have a high BMI despite low body fat. It also does not account for fat distribution, bone density, age, or sex. BMI should be used as a starting point, not a definitive health diagnosis.

TDEE and the Mifflin-St Jeor Equation

Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) represents the total number of calories your body burns in a day, including your Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR), physical activity, and the thermic effect of food. Knowing your TDEE is essential for managing weight: eating below your TDEE creates a caloric deficit (weight loss), while eating above it creates a surplus (weight gain).

Our calculator uses the Mifflin-St Jeor equation, which is considered the most accurate BMR formula for most people. For men: BMR = 10 × weight(kg) + 6.25 × height(cm) − 5 × age − 161 + 166. For women: BMR = 10 × weight(kg) + 6.25 × height(cm) − 5 × age − 161. The BMR is then multiplied by an activity factor (1.2 for sedentary up to 1.9 for extremely active) to estimate your TDEE.

Macronutrient Ratios for Different Goals

Macronutrients — protein, carbohydrates, and fat — are the three main nutrients your body needs in large amounts. The optimal ratio depends on your fitness goals:

  • Weight loss: Higher protein (40%), moderate carbs (30%), moderate fat (30%) helps preserve muscle while in a caloric deficit.
  • Muscle gain: Higher carbs (45%) fuel intense workouts, with adequate protein (30%) for muscle repair and moderate fat (25%).
  • Maintenance: A balanced split of approximately 30% protein, 40% carbs, and 30% fat works for most people.
  • Keto: Very high fat (65–75%), moderate protein (20–30%), and very low carbs (5–10%) to maintain ketosis.

Protein provides 4 calories per gram, carbohydrates provide 4 calories per gram, and fat provides 9 calories per gram. Our calculator converts your calorie target into grams for each macronutrient based on your selected goal.

Heart Rate Zones and Training

Heart rate training zones are ranges based on your maximum heart rate (MHR), estimated as 220 − age. Training in different zones targets different fitness outcomes: Zone 1 (50–60% MHR) for recovery, Zone 2 (60–70%) for fat burning and endurance, Zone 3 (70–80%) for aerobic fitness, Zone 4 (80–90%) for anaerobic threshold, and Zone 5 (90–100%) for maximum performance. Spending time in each zone develops different aspects of cardiovascular fitness.

Frequently Asked Questions

How accurate is the one-rep max (1RM) estimate?

Our calculator averages three validated formulas — Brzycki, Epley, and Lander — to provide a reliable estimate. These formulas are most accurate when using a rep count between 2 and 10. For safety, always attempt a true one-rep max with a spotter.

Should I eat at my TDEE to maintain weight?

Yes, eating at your TDEE should maintain your current weight. For weight loss, a deficit of 300–500 calories per day is generally recommended for sustainable results. For muscle gain, a surplus of 200–400 calories supports growth without excessive fat gain.

Is BMI the best way to measure body fat?

BMI is a convenient screening tool, but it is not the most accurate measure of body fat. Methods like DEXA scans, hydrostatic weighing, and skinfold calipers provide more precise body composition data. Our Health Calculator also offers a body fat percentage estimator using the US Navy method.
